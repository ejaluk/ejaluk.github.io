Phase 01

Set the gates before the game

The project started with a discretionary trading idea that felt profitable. The research question: does it survive once it's made mechanical and measured honestly? Before a single backtest ran, pass/fail criteria were committed — on a simulated $50K account, after realistic spread, commission, and slippage costs.

GateThresholdWhy
Expectancy> $0 / tradePositive expected value after costs — not just a lucky streak.
Max drawdown< 8%A result that survives only by tolerating deep losses isn't a result.
Win rate≥ 55%The breakeven threshold for the strategy's 1:1 risk-reward structure.
Sample size≥ 200 tradesBelow this, performance is indistinguishable from noise. No exceptions.
Principle

Committing the bar before the analysis is the cheapest defense against fooling yourself. The results never got to move the goalposts.

Phase 02

Earn the data before using it

170,000+ rows of financial time-series data across four currency markets and two timeframes — arriving with UTF-16 encoding, broker timezone offsets, and a stretch of legacy history contaminated with mixed-resolution records.

  • One door in. Every dataset flows through a single validated ingestion pipeline — a fix applied once is applied everywhere.
  • A hard quality floor. Contaminated history is blocked unconditionally in code, not by convention or memory.
  • Anomalies investigated, not deleted. Apparent "future-dated" records were diagnosed as a broker timezone artifact — documented, not silently dropped.
  • A firewall around validation data. The hold-out set is untouched by design decisions; any attempted access during research raises an error in code.
Phase 03

Distrust good news

The framework: a Python backtesting engine, a 45-test automated suite held at zero regressions, one change at a time, and counterfactual analysis before any rule change. But the phase is best summarized by one incident:

The "great" result

95.6%
win rate · dev slice

A development run showed one setup winning almost every trade. The tempting move: celebrate and scale up.

The actual finding

A bug.
inverted entry trigger

I halted the pipeline instead — no real strategy wins 95% of the time. Investigation found the entry logic inverted, producing physically impossible fills. Fixed, re-tested, moved on.

Principle

Results that look too good are treated as the most urgent bug reports. Skepticism is applied hardest to the numbers I most want to believe.

Phase 04

The verdict the data gave

The first full strategy generation went through four rounds of backtesting and bug-fixing to reach a clean, trustworthy run: 1,952 trades over 12+ months across four markets. Then it faced the gates.

Hypothesis rejected

The entry concept had no measurable edge — the directional hit rate on clean 1:1 trades was 36.7%, worse than a coin flip. The version was closed with a formal written post-mortem establishing the verified cause of death before any successor was designed.

v1.1 · closed on evidence · post-mortem on file

One counterfactual from this phase mattered more than the headline: the mechanical exit rules had ugly win rates (~21%) and looked like the problem. Testing their removal showed expectancy getting ~4× worse — roughly −$10 to −$40 per trade. They weren't causing losses; they were damage control on bad entries. The entry signal was the disease; the exits were the tourniquet.

Phase 05

Iterate — without flinching

The second generation rebuilt the entry concept around a different structural idea and ran a 19-month, 1,507-trade campaign. The gates didn't soften, and neither did the answers:

Setup A

−$16.08
per trade · 1,421 trades

Structurally negative across every market and every session. Rejected — no filter could rescue a signal that was wrong at the core.

Setup B

+$5.14
per trade · 86 trades

Positive expectancy — but 86 trades is under half the 200-trade floor. Parameter sweeps confirmed frequency couldn't rise without destroying the edge. Ruled statistically insufficient, not "promising."

That Setup B call is the one I'd defend in any interview: a positive number I refused to believe, because the sample size said I hadn't earned the right to believe it. Research continues on the third generation — same gates, same firewall, same discipline.

Why this matters to an employer

It would have been easy to tune parameters until something looked good and declare victory. Rejecting my own hypotheses on pre-committed criteria — three versions in a row — is exactly the discipline I'll bring to your data.
